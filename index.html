Understanding Trauma
Knowledge brings healing
"Then you will know the truth, and the truth will set you free."
- John 8:32
What is Trauma?
Trauma is the result of events that are emotionally disturbing or life-threatening. Sexual trauma specifically affects your sense of safety, trust, and personal boundaries. Understanding this is key to healing.
Common Trauma Responses
Hypervigilance, flashbacks, nightmares, emotional numbing, and physical symptoms are normal responses to abnormal experiences. Your body is trying to protect you.
Trauma and Faith
Many survivors struggle with spiritual questions after trauma. These doubts are normal and don't reflect a lack of faith. God can handle your questions and anger.
Healing is Possible
Recovery from sexual trauma is possible with proper support, therapy, and time. Healing doesn't mean forgetting - it means the trauma no longer controls your life.
Supporting Others
Learn how to support other survivors or how to talk to loved ones about your own healing journey. Community is essential for recovery.

Understanding Trauma
What happens when we experience trauma
"The Lord is close to the brokenhearted and saves those who are crushed in spirit."
- Psalm 34:18
What is Sexual Trauma?
Sexual trauma occurs when someone experiences a sexual act against their will. This includes rape, sexual assault, childhood sexual abuse, intimate partner sexual violence, and sexual harassment. The impact goes far beyond the physical - it affects emotions, relationships, spirituality, and sense of safety.
Normal Responses to Abnormal Events
Your reactions to trauma are normal responses to abnormal events. These may include:
‚Ä¢ Physical: Sleep problems, headaches, stomach issues, fatigue
‚Ä¢ Emotional: Fear, anger, sadness, numbness, mood swings
‚Ä¢ Mental: Flashbacks, nightmares, difficulty concentrating, memory problems
‚Ä¢ Behavioral: Avoiding triggers, changes in eating, social withdrawal
‚Ä¢ Spiritual: Questioning God, feeling abandoned, struggling with faith
The Brain and Trauma
Trauma literally changes the brain. The amygdala (alarm system) becomes hyperactive while the prefrontal cortex (rational thinking) goes offline. This is why you might feel "crazy" or like you're overreacting. Your brain is doing exactly what it's designed to do - protect you from danger.
It's Not Your Fault
Sexual trauma is NEVER the victim's fault. Regardless of what you were wearing, where you were, what you said, whether you knew the person, or any other factor - the responsibility lies completely with the perpetrator. God does not blame you for what someone else chose to do.
Common Trauma Responses
Understanding your body's protective reactions
"My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever."
- Psalm 73:26
Fight, Flight, Freeze, or Fawn
Fight: Becoming aggressive, argumentative, or confrontational
Flight: Wanting to run away, feeling restless, avoiding situations
Freeze: Feeling paralyzed, unable to move or speak, dissociation
Fawn: People-pleasing, trying to appease others, losing your voice
Hypervigilance
Constantly scanning for danger, being easily startled, difficulty relaxing, feeling like you need to have your back to the wall. This exhausting state of alertness is your brain trying to keep you safe.
Flashbacks and Triggers
Flashbacks can involve all five senses and make you feel like the trauma is happening again. Triggers can be sights, sounds, smells, touches, or situations that remind your body of the trauma. Understanding triggers helps you prepare and respond with self-compassion.
Dissociation
Feeling disconnected from your body, emotions, or surroundings. You might feel like you're watching yourself from outside your body or like things aren't real. Dissociation was a survival mechanism that helped you endure trauma.
Emotional Numbing
Sometimes the brain shuts down emotions to protect you from overwhelming pain. While this can be concerning, it's a normal protective response. Emotions will return as you heal and feel safer.
Trauma and Faith
Wrestling with God after trauma
"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me? Why are you so far from saving me, so far from my cries of anguish?"
- Psalm 22:1
Common Spiritual Questions
‚Ä¢ Where was God when this happened?
‚Ä¢ Why didn't God protect me?
‚Ä¢ Am I being punished for something?
‚Ä¢ Is this part of God's plan?
‚Ä¢ How can a loving God allow such evil?
‚Ä¢ Am I still pure/worthy of God's love?
These questions are normal and don't indicate weak faith.
God Can Handle Your Anger
Many Psalms express anger at God (Psalm 13, 22, 88). Job questioned God extensively. Jesus himself cried out in anguish. God is big enough to handle your pain, questions, and even anger. Honest communication with God is the foundation of authentic relationship.
Spiritual Abuse and Religious Trauma
Sometimes religious leaders or communities respond to trauma with harmful theology: "God allows everything for a reason," "You need to forgive and move on," "If you had enough faith, you'd be healed." These responses can cause additional trauma and don't reflect God's heart.
God's Heart for Trauma Survivors
God is particularly close to the brokenhearted (Psalm 34:18). Christ came to bind up the brokenhearted and set captives free (Isaiah 61:1). Your trauma grieves God's heart. You are not damaged goods - you are God's beloved child, precious and honored in His sight.
Healing is Possible
Hope for your journey
"And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose."
- Romans 8:28
What Healing Looks Like
Healing doesn't mean forgetting or "getting over it." Healing means the trauma no longer controls your life. You can remember without being overwhelmed. You can feel safe again. You can trust again. You can experience joy and peace. The wound becomes a scar - still there, but no longer bleeding.
Healing is Not Linear
Some days will be better than others. Progress isn't always visible. Setbacks don't mean you're not healing. Think of healing like a spiral staircase - sometimes you face the same direction as before, but you're on a higher level. Be patient and gentle with yourself.
Post-Traumatic Growth
Many survivors experience growth beyond their pre-trauma functioning. This might include deeper relationships, increased compassion, stronger faith, clearer priorities, greater appreciation for life, and a sense of personal strength. Your pain can become a source of purpose and ministry to others.
Beauty from Ashes
God specializes in redemption - bringing beauty from ashes, joy from mourning, praise from despair. Your story isn't over. The enemy meant your trauma for evil, but God can use it for good. Your healing can become a beacon of hope for other survivors.

Secondary Trauma
Supporting trauma survivors can affect your own mental health. Watch for signs of secondary trauma: intrusive thoughts about their story, difficulty sleeping, increased anxiety, feeling overwhelmed. Practice self-care and seek support when needed.
Know Your Limits
You are not responsible for someone's healing - that's work for professional therapists and ultimately God. You can offer friendship, prayer, and practical support, but you cannot and should not try to be their counselor. Encourage professional help when needed.
Trauma-Informed Christian Therapy
Finding professional help that honors faith
"Plans fail for lack of counsel, but with many advisers they succeed."
- Proverbs 15:22
EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)
Highly effective for processing traumatic memories. Uses bilateral stimulation to help the brain process trauma naturally. Many Christian EMDR therapists integrate prayer and faith into treatment.
CPT (Cognitive Processing Therapy)
Helps challenge and change unhelpful thoughts about the trauma. Particularly effective for self-blame and shame. Can be adapted to include biblical truth and Christian cognitive restructuring.
DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy)
Teaches practical skills for managing emotions, tolerating distress, and improving relationships. Many skills align with biblical principles of self-control and emotional regulation.
Somatic Approaches
Focus on how trauma is stored in the body. Includes techniques like breathing exercises, grounding, and body awareness. Can be integrated with understanding the body as God's temple.
